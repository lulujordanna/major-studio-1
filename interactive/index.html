Undress for the Met

30 years since the inception of The Guerrilla Girls infamous poster, "Do Women Have To Be Naked To Get Into The Met Museum?", I began to question how has The Metropolitan Museum progressed? Are female nudes still predominately on display? Since 1989, is The Met collecting more female artists? Through this data visualization, I explore the guiding questions and pay homage to The Guerrilla Girls who paved this path.

2019

Less that 14% of the artists in the Modern Art Collection are women, but 60% of the nudes are female.

Collecting nudes

The graph above compares the solo male and female nudes in the Met's collection. In Solo female nudes represent 60% of the collection, while solo male nudes represent 28% of the collection. Nudes with both genders represent 11% of the collection but were excluding in this comparative analysis. When the bar graph is rescaled, the largest decade for collecting female nudes in the 1980s (258 artifacts), followed by the 2000s (158 artifacts) after The Guerilla Girls, "Do Women Have To Be Naked To Get Into The Met Museum?" was created. Female artists on the rise?

The graph above breaks down the artist's gender of new acquisitions in the Modern Art Collection since 1989. Exploring the data, in the past 30 years the acquisitions have skewed towards male artists. The most objects by female artists were collected in 2009 (35 objects), when the collection acquired 11 pieces from the textile artist, Doris Tillett.

Methodology

This visualization was built through HTML, CSS, and D3. Special thanks to The Metropolitan Museum of Art's Digital Collection Open Access and Clare Churchouse for providing the data sources. The total sample size for the nude comparative analysis is 2011 objects and for the Modern Art acquisitions is 5035 objects. All photos are Public Domain images provided through The Metropolitan Museum of Art's Digital Collection Open Access.
